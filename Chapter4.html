Chapter 4: Electrochemistry

4.1 Electrolysis

1: Define electrolysis as the decomposition of an ionic compound, when molten or in aqueous solution, by the passage of an electric current
Electrolysis: The chemical decomposition (breaking down) of an ionic compoundCompound containing positive and negative ions, typically formed between metals and non-metals., either when it is molten (melted) or dissolved in water (aqueous solution), caused by passing a direct electric current through it. This process requires the ions to be mobile (free to move).
2: Identify in simple electrolytic cells: (a) the anode as the positive electrode (b) the cathode as the negative electrode (c) the electrolyte as the molten or aqueous substance that undergoes electrolysis

Components of a simple electrolytic cell:

  • Electrodes: Conductors through which electricity enters or leaves the electrolyte. Often made of inert materials like graphite (carbon) or platinum, unless they are intended to react (active electrodes).
    • (a) Anode: The positive (+) electrode. It attracts anions (negative ions). Oxidation occurs here. (Mnemonic: AnOx - Anode Oxidation).
    • (b) Cathode: The negative (-) electrode. It attracts cations (positive ions). Reduction occurs here. (Mnemonic: RedCat - Reduction Cathode).
  • (c) Electrolyte: The molten ionic compound or an aqueous solution containing ions. The ions must be mobile to conduct electricity and undergo reaction.
  • Power Supply: Provides the direct current (DC) necessary to drive the non-spontaneous reactions. Electrons flow from the negative terminal of the power supply to the cathode, and from the anode to the positive terminal.

8: Describe the transfer of charge during electrolysis to include: (a) the movement of electrons in the external circuit (b) the loss or gain of electrons at the electrodes (c) the movement of ions in the electrolyte

Charge Transfer Mechanism

  1. (a) External Circuit: The power supply pushes electrons. Electrons flow from the negative terminal, through the wire, to the cathode. Electrons flow away from the anode, through the wire, back to the positive terminal of the power supply. (Charge carrier: electrons)
  2. (b) Electrodes (Redox Reactions):
    • At the Cathode (-): Positive ions (cations) from the electrolyte are attracted. They gain electrons supplied by the external circuit (Reduction). E.g., \( \text{M}^{n+}(aq/l) + ne^- \rightarrow \text{M}(s/l) \) or \( 2H^+(aq) + 2e^- \rightarrow H_2(g) \).
    • At the Anode (+): Negative ions (anions) from the electrolyte are attracted (or sometimes the electrode material itself reacts). They lose electrons to the external circuit (Oxidation). E.g., \( \text{X}^{m-}(aq/l) \rightarrow \text{X} + me^- \) or \( 4OH^-(aq) \rightarrow O_2(g) + 2H_2O(l) + 4e^- \).
  3. (c) Electrolyte: Mobile ions move to maintain electrical neutrality and complete the circuit. Cations (+) move towards the cathode (-). Anions (-) move towards the anode (+). (Charge carrier: ions)
Key Idea: The circuit is completed by electron flow in the wires and ion flow in the electrolyte, linked by redox reactions at the electrode surfaces.
3: Identify the products formed at the electrodes and describe the observations made during the electrolysis of: (a) molten lead(II) bromide (b) concentrated aqueous sodium chloride (c) dilute sulfuric acid using inert electrodes made of platinum or carbon / graphite
These examples use Inert Electrodes (Carbon/Graphite or Platinum), which do not participate in the reaction.

(a) Molten Lead(II) Bromide (PbBr₂)

Electrolyte: Molten PbBr₂, contains mobile \(Pb^{2+}(l)\) and \(Br^{-}(l)\) ions.

Cathode (-): Attracts \(Pb^{2+}\).
Reaction: \(Pb^{2+}(l) + 2e^- \rightarrow Pb(l)\).
Observation: Silvery, molten metal (lead) forms at the cathode.

Anode (+): Attracts \(Br^{-}\).
Reaction: \(2Br^-(l) \rightarrow Br_2(g) + 2e^-\).
Observation: Brown/orange vapour or gas (bromine) evolves at the anode.

(b) Concentrated Aqueous Sodium Chloride (NaCl(aq))

Electrolyte: Contains \(Na^+(aq)\), \(Cl^-(aq)\) from NaCl, and \(H^+(aq)\), \(OH^-(aq)\) from water dissociation.

Cathode (-): Attracts \(Na^+\) and \(H^+\). H⁺ is less reactive (easier to reduce) than Na⁺.
Reaction: \(2H^+(aq) + 2e^- \rightarrow H_2(g)\).
Observation: Bubbles of colourless gas (hydrogen) evolve at the cathode. Test: Lit splint gives a 'pop'.

Anode (+): Attracts \(Cl^-\) and \(OH^-\). In CONCENTRATED solution, Cl⁻ is preferentially discharged over OH⁻.
Reaction: \(2Cl^-(aq) \rightarrow Cl_2(g) + 2e^-\).
Observation: Bubbles of pale green gas (chlorine) evolve at the anode. Test: Bleaches damp blue litmus paper.

Remaining Solution: \(Na^+(aq)\) and \(OH^-(aq)\) ions remain, forming sodium hydroxide solution, which is alkaline (pH increases).

(c) Dilute Sulfuric Acid (H₂SO₄(aq))

Electrolyte: Contains \(H^+(aq)\) and \(SO_4^{2-}(aq)\) from the acid, and also \(H^+(aq)\) and \(OH^-(aq)\) from water. Primarily \(H^+\) and \(OH^-\) ions from water are involved.

Cathode (-): Attracts \(H^+\).
Reaction: \(2H^+(aq) + 2e^- \rightarrow H_2(g)\).
Observation: Bubbles of colourless gas (hydrogen) evolve at the cathode.

Anode (+): Attracts \(OH^-\) and \(SO_4^{2-}\). OH⁻ is preferentially discharged over the stable sulfate ion (SO₄²⁻).
Reaction: \(4OH^-(aq) \rightarrow O_2(g) + 2H_2O(l) + 4e^-\) (or simplified from water: \(2H_2O(l) \rightarrow O_2(g) + 4H^+(aq) + 4e^-\)).
Observation: Bubbles of colourless gas (oxygen) evolve at the anode. Test: Relights a glowing splint.

Overall Effect: Water is decomposed. Volume ratio of H₂:O₂ produced is 2:1. Acid concentration increases slightly as water is removed.

4: State that metals or hydrogen are formed at the cathode and that non-metals (other than hydrogen) are formed at the anode

General Rule Summary (Inert Electrodes):

  • At the Cathode (-): Always involves reduction (gain of electrons). Either metal ions are reduced to metal atoms (if metal is less reactive than hydrogen) OR hydrogen ions (from water or acid) are reduced to hydrogen gas (if metal is more reactive than hydrogen).
  • At the Anode (+): Always involves oxidation (loss of electrons). Either non-metal ions (like halides) are oxidised to the non-metal element OR hydroxide ions (from water) are oxidised to oxygen gas. (Certain complex ions like SO₄²⁻, NO₃⁻ are generally not discharged in aqueous solution).
5: Predict the identity of the products at each electrode for the electrolysis of a binary compound in the molten state

For a simple binary ionic compound (containing only two elements, one metal and one non-metal) in the molten state:

  • Cathode (-): The positive metal ion (cation) is reduced to the molten metal element. E.g., for molten MgCl₂, \(Mg^{2+} + 2e^- \rightarrow Mg(l)\).
  • Anode (+): The negative non-metal ion (anion) is oxidised to the non-metal element. E.g., for molten MgCl₂, \(2Cl^- \rightarrow Cl_2(g) + 2e^-\).
9: Identify the products formed at the electrodes and describe the observations made during the electrolysis of aqueous copper(II) sulfate using inert carbon / graphite electrodes and when using copper electrodes

Electrolysis of Aqueous Copper(II) Sulfate (CuSO₄(aq))

Electrolyte: Contains \(Cu^{2+}(aq)\) (blue ions), \(SO_4^{2-}(aq)\) (colourless), \(H^+(aq)\) (colourless), \(OH^-(aq)\) (colourless).

Results for Inert Electrodes shown below.
Using Inert (Carbon/Graphite) Electrodes:

Cathode (-): Attracts \(Cu^{2+}\) and \(H^+\). Cu is less reactive than H, so Cu²⁺ is preferentially discharged.
Reaction: \(Cu^{2+}(aq) + 2e^- \rightarrow Cu(s)\).
Observation: Pink/brown solid (copper) deposited on the cathode.

Anode (+): Attracts \(OH^-\) and \(SO_4^{2-}\). OH⁻ is discharged in preference to SO₄²⁻.
Reaction: \(4OH^-(aq) \rightarrow O_2(g) + 2H_2O(l) + 4e^-\).
Observation: Bubbles of colourless gas (oxygen) form at the anode.

Electrolyte Change: Blue colour fades as \(Cu^{2+}\) ions are removed. \(H^+\) and \(SO_4^{2-}\) ions remain/build up, making the solution acidic (sulfuric acid forms).

Using Copper (Active) Electrodes:

Cathode (-): Same as inert: Attracts \(Cu^{2+}\) and \(H^+\). Cu²⁺ is preferentially discharged.
Reaction: \(Cu^{2+}(aq) + 2e^- \rightarrow Cu(s)\).
Observation: Pink/brown solid (copper) deposited on the cathode.

Anode (+): Attracts \(OH^-\), \(SO_4^{2-}\), but the copper anode itself is easier to oxidise.
Reaction: \(Cu(s) \rightarrow Cu^{2+}(aq) + 2e^-\).
Observation: The copper anode dissolves/gets smaller.

Electrolyte Change: \(Cu^{2+}\) ions are removed at the cathode but replenished by the anode dissolving. Therefore, the concentration of \(Cu^{2+}\) (and the blue colour) remains approximately constant. Used for refining copper.

Predicting Products in Aqueous Halide Solutions

  • Cathode (-): Always compare the reactivity of the metal cation (from the halide salt) with Hydrogen (H⁺ from water).
    • If Metal is MORE reactive than H (e.g., Na, K, Ca, Mg, Al, Zn, Fe): Hydrogen gas (H₂) is produced. \(2H^+(aq) + 2e^- \rightarrow H_2(g)\).
    • If Metal is LESS reactive than H (e.g., Cu, Ag, Au): The Metal is deposited. \( \text{Metal}^{n+}(aq) + ne^- \rightarrow \text{Metal}(s) \).
  • Anode (+): Compare the ease of discharge of the halide ion (X⁻) versus the hydroxide ion (OH⁻ from water).
    • If solution is CONCENTRATED Halide: The Halide ion (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) is usually discharged → Halogen gas (Cl₂, Br₂, I₂) forms. \(2X^-(aq) \rightarrow X_2(g/l/s) + 2e^-\).
    • If solution is DILUTE Halide (or contains F⁻ or SO₄²⁻/NO₃⁻): Hydroxide ion (OH⁻) is discharged → Oxygen gas (O₂) forms. \(4OH^-(aq) \rightarrow O_2(g) + 2H_2O(l) + 4e^-\).
    • (Note: Fluoride ions, F⁻, are very difficult to oxidise, so O₂ is always produced from aqueous fluoride solutions).

11: Construct ionic half-equations for reactions at the anode (to show oxidation) and at the cathode (to show reduction)

Constructing Ionic Half-Equations

These equations show explicitly the species reacting and the electrons gained or lost.

  • Balance atoms first.
  • Balance charge by adding electrons (e⁻) to the appropriate side.
  • Include state symbols.

Examples:

  • Reduction at Cathode (-):
  • Lead ions: \(Pb^{2+}(l) + 2e^- \rightarrow Pb(l)\)
  • Hydrogen ions: \(2H^{+}(aq) + 2e^- \rightarrow H_2(g)\)
  • Copper(II) ions: \(Cu^{2+}(aq) + 2e^- \rightarrow Cu(s)\)
  • Aluminium ions: \(Al^{3+}(l) + 3e^- \rightarrow Al(l)\)
  • Oxidation at Anode (+):
  • Bromide ions: \(2Br^-(l/aq) \rightarrow Br_2(g/l) + 2e^-\)
  • Chloride ions: \(2Cl^-(aq) \rightarrow Cl_2(g) + 2e^-\)
  • Hydroxide ions: \(4OH^-(aq) \rightarrow O_2(g) + 2H_2O(l) + 4e^-\)
  • Copper metal (active anode): \(Cu(s) \rightarrow Cu^{2+}(aq) + 2e^-\)
6: State that metal objects are electroplated to improve their appearance and resistance to corrosion
7: Describe how metals are electroplated

Electroplating

Electroplating is a process that uses electrolysis to coat an object (usually metallic) with a thin layer of another metal.

Purposes:

  1. Improve Appearance: Make objects look more attractive (e.g., silver plating cutlery, chromium plating car parts).
  2. Improve Corrosion Resistance: Protect the underlying metal from rusting or corroding (e.g., zinc plating steel - galvanising, chromium plating taps).

Method / Setup:

  1. The object to be plated is made the Cathode (-) of the electrolytic cell.
  2. The metal used for plating (e.g., silver, copper, chromium) is made the Anode (+) (this is an active electrode).
  3. The electrolyte is an aqueous solution containing ions of the plating metal (e.g., silver nitrate solution for silver plating, copper(II) sulfate solution for copper plating).
  4. A direct current is passed through the cell.

Silver plating an iron spoon

Setup: Iron Spoon = Cathode (-). Pure Silver Bar = Anode (+). Electrolyte = Silver Nitrate Solution (\(AgNO_3(aq)\)).

Reactions:

  • Anode (+): Silver metal dissolves to form ions: \(Ag(s) \rightarrow Ag^{+}(aq) + e^-\).
  • Cathode (-): Silver ions from solution deposit onto the spoon: \(Ag^{+}(aq) + e^- \rightarrow Ag(s)\).

Result: The iron spoon gets coated with a layer of silver. The silver anode gradually dissolves, replenishing the silver ions in the electrolyte.

4.2 Hydrogen–oxygen fuel cells

1: State that a hydrogen–oxygen fuel cell uses hydrogen and oxygen to produce electricity with water as the only chemical product
Hydrogen–Oxygen Fuel Cell: An electrochemical device that continuously converts the chemical energy from the reaction between hydrogen (\(H_2\)) and oxygen (\(O_2\)) directly into electricity. The only chemical product formed in the overall process is water (\(H_2O\)).

Overall Reaction: \(2H_2(g) + O_2(g) \rightarrow 2H_2O(l/g)\) + Electrical Energy

(Note: The reactions occur via separate electrode processes involving ions and electrons, but the net result is the combination of H₂ and O₂ to form water).

2: Describe the advantages and disadvantages of using hydrogen–oxygen fuel cells in comparison with gasoline / petrol engines in vehicles

Fuel Cells vs. Petrol Engines Comparison

Hydrogen-Oxygen Fuel Cell Gasoline/Petrol Engine
Advantages:
  • No Pollution at Source: Only water produced; no greenhouse gases (CO₂) or toxic pollutants (CO, NOx, particulates) emitted by the vehicle itself.
  • High Efficiency: Convert chemical energy directly to electrical energy more efficiently than internal combustion engines (less energy wasted as heat).
  • Quiet Operation: Less noise pollution.
Disadvantages:
  • Hydrogen Production: Most H₂ is currently made from fossil fuels (releasing CO₂) or requires significant electricity for electrolysis (which might come from fossil fuels). Truly 'green' H₂ is expensive.
  • Hydrogen Storage: H₂ is a low-density gas, difficult and bulky to store safely on vehicles (requires high pressure tanks or very low temperatures).
  • Cost: Fuel cells often use expensive catalysts (like platinum) and the overall technology cost is high.
  • Infrastructure: Limited network of hydrogen refueling stations compared to petrol stations.
 Advantages:
  • Mature Technology: Well-established manufacturing and repair infrastructure. Lower initial vehicle cost.
  • Extensive Refueling Infrastructure: Petrol stations are widespread.
  • Fuel Handling: Liquid petrol is relatively easy and safe to store and transport. High energy density per volume.
Disadvantages:
  • Pollution: Produces greenhouse gases (CO₂), toxic pollutants (CO, NOx, unburnt hydrocarbons, particulates) contributing to air pollution and climate change.
  • Lower Efficiency: Significant energy loss as heat during combustion.
  • Noise Pollution.
  • Finite Resource: Relies on non-renewable fossil fuels. Price volatility.
