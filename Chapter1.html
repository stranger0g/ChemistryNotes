2. Describe the structures of solids, liquids, and gases in terms of particle separation, arrangement, and motion

The kinetic particle theory describes matter as composed of particles in constant motion.

  • Solids: Particles closely packed in a fixed, regular arrangement. Strong intermolecular forces. Particles vibrate about fixed positions (low KE).
  • Liquids: Particles closely packed, randomly arranged. Weaker intermolecular forces. Particles slide past one another (moderate KE).
  • Gases: Particles far apart, random arrangement. Negligible intermolecular forces. Particles move rapidly and randomly (high KE).

3. Describe changes of state in terms of melting, boiling, evaporating, freezing and condensing

Transitions between phases:

  • Melting: Solid → Liquid (at melting point, energy absorbed).
  • Freezing: Liquid → Solid (at freezing point, energy released).
  • Boiling: Liquid → Gas (throughout, at boiling point, energy absorbed).
  • Evaporation: Liquid → Gas (surface only, below boiling point, energy absorbed).
  • Condensing: Gas → Liquid (energy released).
  • Sublimation:Direct change from solid to gas. Solid → Gas (energy absorbed). E.g., Iodine, CO₂ (dry ice).
  • Deposition:Direct change from gas to solid. Gas → Solid (energy released). E.g., Frost formation.
5. Explain changes of state in terms of kinetic particle theory, including the interpretation of heating and cooling curves

Energy input changes particle KE (temp) or PE (overcoming forces during state change).

  • Kinetic Theory: Temp = average KE. State changes involve overcoming intermolecular forces (potential energy change) using Latent Heat (of Fusion/Vaporization).
  • Heating Curve: Shows temperature vs. time/heat added.
    • Sloped sections: KE increases, Temp rises (single state).
    • Flat sections (plateaus): State change occurs, PE increases (overcoming forces), Temp constant (Melting/Boiling Point).
  • Cooling Curve: Opposite of heating curve (slopes down, plateaus at condensing/freezing points).

4. Describe the effects of temperature and pressure on the volume of a gas
  • Temp Effect (const P): ↑Temperature → ↑Volume. (Particles move faster, hit walls harder/more often, expand to maintain pressure).
  • Pressure Effect (const T): ↑Pressure → ↓Volume. (Particles forced closer together).
6. Explain, in terms of kinetic particle theory, the effects of temperature and pressure on the volume of a gas
  • Temp Explanation (const P): ↑Temp → ↑Average KE → particles move faster/collide with walls more frequently and forcefully → Volume must increase to keep collision rate/force per unit area (Pressure) constant.
  • Pressure Explanation (const T): ↑External Pressure pushes particles closer → ↓Volume → particles collide with walls more frequently in smaller space → Internal pressure increases until it matches external pressure.

1.2 Diffusion

1. Describe and explain diffusion in terms of kinetic particle theory

Diffusion: Net movement of particles from region of higher concentration to lower concentration (down a concentration gradient) due to random motion of particles.

Explanation: Particles are in constant, random motion. Where concentration is high, more particles move away from that region than towards it, resulting in net movement to lower concentration areas until evenly mixed.

2. Describe and explain the effect of relative molecular mass (Mr) on the rate of diffusion of gases

Rate of diffusion depends on particle mass.

  • At the same temperature, all gases have the same average kinetic energy (\(KE = ½mv²\)).
  • Therefore, lighter particles (lower Mr / lower mass 'm') must have higher average speed ('v') to have the same KE.
  • ∴ Gases with lower Mr diffuse faster because their particles move faster on average.

Example: HCl vs NH₃ Diffusion

Ammonia (\(NH_3\), \(M_r \approx 17\)) diffuses faster than Hydrogen Chloride (\(HCl\), \(M_r \approx 36.5\)). In the classic tube experiment, the white ring of \(NH_4Cl\) forms closer to the HCl end.
