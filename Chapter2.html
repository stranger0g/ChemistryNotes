Central nucleus (containing protons & neutrons) surrounded by electrons orbiting in specific energy levels or shells.

3. Define proton number / atomic number (Z) as the number of protons in the nucleus of an atom

Proton number (Z): Number of protons in the nucleus. It uniquely identifies an element. In a neutral atom, the number of electrons equals the number of protons.

4. Define mass number / nucleon number (A) as the total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus of an atom

Mass number (A): Total number of protons + neutrons in the nucleus. \( A = Z + N \), where N is the number of neutrons. Therefore, the number of neutrons \(N = A - Z\).

5. Determine the electronic configuration of elements and their ions with proton number 1 to 20, e.g. 2,8,3

Electronic configuration: Arrangement of electrons in shells.

  • Shells fill from inside: 1st shell (max 2 e-), 2nd shell (max 8 e-), 3rd shell (max 8 e- for Z≤20).

Examples (Neutral Atoms):

  • Li (Z=3): 2,1
  • Ne (Z=10): 2,8
  • Na (Z=11): 2,8,1
  • Ar (Z=18): 2,8,8
  • K (Z=19): 2,8,8,1
  • Ca (Z=20): 2,8,8,2

Ions: Formed to achieve a stable electron configuration (usually a full outer shell like noble gases).

  • Cations (+ve): Lose outer shell e-. E.g., Mg (2,8,2) loses 2e- → Mg2+ ion (2,8).
  • Anions (-ve): Gain e- to fill outer shell. E.g., O (2,6) gains 2e- → O2- ion (2,8).
6. State that: (a) Group VIII noble gases have a full outer shell (b) the number of outer shell electrons is equal to the group number in Groups I to VII (c) the number of occupied electron shells is equal to the period number
  • (a) Noble Gases (Group VIII/0): Have a full outer electron shell (2 for He, 8 for others up to Ar), making them very stable and unreactive.
  • (b) Groups I-VII: For elements in these groups, the Group Number = Number of electrons in the outer shell. (e.g., Group 1 metals have 1 outer e-, Group VII halogens have 7 outer e-).
  • (c) Period Number: The Period Number = Number of occupied electron shells. (e.g., Period 2 elements Li to Ne have 2 occupied shells, Period 3 elements Na to Ar have 3 occupied shells).

2.3 Isotopes

1. Define isotopes as different atoms of the same element that have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons

Isotopes: Atoms of the same element (meaning they have the same number of protons, Z) but with different numbers of neutrons (meaning they have different mass numbers, A).

Example: Hydrogen Isotopes - All have Z=1.

  • Protium (\( ^{1}_{1}H \)): 1 proton, 0 neutrons.
  • Deuterium (\( ^{2}_{1}H \)): 1 proton, 1 neutron.
  • Tritium (\( ^{3}_{1}H \)): 1 proton, 2 neutrons.
2. Interpret and use symbols for atoms, e.g. \( ^{12}_{6}\text{C} \), and ions, e.g. \( ^{35}_{17}\text{Cl}^- \)

Notation \( ^{A}_{Z}\text{X}^{\text{charge}} \):

  • A = Mass number (protons + neutrons) - Top left.
  • Z = Proton number (atomic number) - Bottom left.
  • X = Chemical symbol of the element.
  • Charge - Top right (omitted if neutral).

Examples:

  • \( ^{12}_{6}\text{C} \): Carbon atom, 6 protons, (12-6)=6 neutrons, 6 electrons (neutral).
  • \( ^{37}_{17}\text{Cl} \): Chlorine atom, 17 protons, (37-17)=20 neutrons, 17 electrons (neutral).
  • \( ^{24}_{12}\text{Mg}^{2+} \): Magnesium ion, 12 protons, (24-12)=12 neutrons, (12-2)=10 electrons (+2 charge means 2 electrons lost).
  • \( ^{35}_{17}\text{Cl}^{-} \): Chloride ion, 17 protons, (35-17)=18 neutrons, (17+1)=18 electrons (-1 charge means 1 electron gained).
3. State that isotopes of the same element have the same chemical properties because they have the same number of electrons and therefore the same electronic configuration

Chemical properties are determined primarily by the arrangement of electrons, particularly the outer shell electrons. Since isotopes of an element have the same number of protons, they also have the same number of electrons and thus the same electronic configuration. This leads to identical chemical behavior.

(Note: Physical properties like density or rate of diffusion may differ slightly due to the mass difference).

4. Calculate the relative atomic mass of an element from the relative masses and the relative abundances of its isotopes

Relative atomic mass (\(A_r\)): The weighted average mass of the isotopes of an element relative to 1/12th of the mass of a carbon-12 atom.

Calculation Formula:

$$ A_r = \frac{(\text{mass}_1 \times \% \text{abundance}_1) + (\text{mass}_2 \times \% \text{abundance}_2) + \dots}{100} $$

Example: Chlorine Calculation

Chlorine exists as \( ^{35}\text{Cl} \) (75% abundance) and \( ^{37}\text{Cl} \) (25% abundance).

$$ A_r(Cl) = \frac{(35 \times 75) + (37 \times 25)}{100} = \frac{2625 + 925}{100} = \frac{3550}{100} = 35.5 $$

Example: Boron Calculation

Boron exists as \( ^{10}\text{B} \) (20% abundance) and \( ^{11}\text{B} \) (80% abundance).

$$ A_r(B) = \frac{(10 \times 20) + (11 \times 80)}{100} = \frac{200 + 880}{100} = \frac{1080}{100} = 10.8 $$

2.4 Ions and Ionic Bonds

1. Describe the formation of positive ions, known as cations, and negative ions, known as anions

Ions are formed when atoms gain or lose electrons to achieve a stable electron configuration (usually a full outer shell).

  • Cations (Positive Ions): Formed when metal atoms lose their outer shell electrons. They now have more protons (+) than electrons (-), resulting in a positive charge. E.g., Na (2,8,1) → Na+ (2,8) + e-. Common cations: Group I (+1), Group II (+2), Al (+3).
  • Anions (Negative Ions): Formed when non-metal atoms gain electrons to fill their outer shell. They now have more electrons (-) than protons (+), resulting in a negative charge. E.g., Cl (2,8,7) + e- → Cl- (2,8,8). Common anions: Group VI (-2), Group VII (-1).
2. State that an ionic bond is a strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions

Ionic bond: The strong electrostatic force of attraction that exists between oppositely charged ions (cations and anions) formed by the transfer of electrons.

3. Describe the formation of ionic bonds between elements from Group I and Group VII, including the use of dot-and-cross diagrams

Example: Sodium (Group I) reacts with Chlorine (Group VII).

  1. Sodium atom (Na: 2,8,1) loses its one outer electron to become a stable sodium ion (Na+: 2,8).
  2. Chlorine atom (Cl: 2,8,7) gains one electron to fill its outer shell, becoming a stable chloride ion (Cl-: 2,8,8).
  3. The oppositely charged Na+ and Cl- ions are strongly attracted to each other by electrostatic forces, forming the ionic bond in Sodium Chloride (NaCl).

Dot-and-Cross Diagram: NaCl Formation (Outer Shells Only)

6. Describe the formation of ionic bonds between ions of metallic and non-metallic elements, including the use of dot-and-cross diagrams

Ionic bonds generally form between metals (which lose electrons) and non-metals (which gain electrons).

Example: Magnesium (Group II) and Oxygen (Group VI)

  1. Mg (2,8,2) loses 2 outer e- → Mg2+ (2,8).
  2. O (2,6) gains 2 outer e- → O2- (2,8).
  3. Ions Mg2+ and O2- attract to form MgO.

Dot-and-Cross Diagram: MgO Formation (Outer Shells Only)

Example: Calcium (Group II) and Fluorine (Group VII)

  1. Ca (2,8,8,2) loses 2 outer e- → Ca2+ (2,8,8).
  2. Each F (2,7) gains 1 outer e- → F- (2,8).
  3. One Ca2+ ion attracts two F- ions to form CaF2. Dot-and-cross diagram would show one Ca transferring one electron to each of two F atoms.
5. Describe the giant lattice structure of ionic compounds as a regular arrangement of alternating positive and negative ions

Giant ionic lattice: A regular, repeating three-dimensional arrangement of a very large number of alternating positive (cations) and negative (anions) ions, held together by strong electrostatic forces acting in all directions.

Simplified Ionic Lattice (e.g., NaCl type)

4. Describe the properties of ionic compounds: (a) high melting points and boiling points (b) good electrical conductivity when aqueous or molten and poor when solid
  • (a) High Melting/Boiling Points: Ionic compounds are typically solids at room temperature with very high melting and boiling points.
  • (b) Electrical Conductivity: They conduct electricity when molten (liquid) or dissolved in water (aqueous), because the ions are free to move and carry charge. They do not conduct electricity when solid, because the ions are held in fixed positions within the lattice.
  • Usually crystalline solids, often soluble in water (polar solvent).
7. Explain the properties of ionic compounds in terms of structure and bonding: (a) high melting points and boiling points (b) good electrical conductivity when aqueous or molten and poor when solid
  • (a) High MP/BP Explained: A large amount of thermal energy is required to overcome the strong, multi-directional electrostatic forces of attraction between the oppositely charged ions throughout the giant ionic lattice.
  • (b) Electrical Conductivity Explained: Conduction requires mobile charge carriers. In solid ionic compounds, ions are fixed in the lattice and cannot move. When molten or dissolved, the ions become mobile and can move towards the electrodes, carrying charge and allowing conduction.

2.5 Simple Molecules and Covalent Bonds

1. State that a covalent bond is formed when a pair of electrons is shared between two atoms leading to noble gas electronic configurations

Covalent bond: A chemical bond formed by the sharing of one or more pairs of electrons (usually between non-metal atoms) so that each atom achieves a stable electron configuration, often resembling that of a noble gas (e.g., a full outer shell).

2. Describe the formation of covalent bonds in simple molecules, including H₂, Cl₂, H₂O, CH₄, NH₃ and HCl. Use dot-and-cross diagrams to show the electronic configurations in these and similar molecules

Atoms joined covalently form molecules. Simple molecules contain a small, fixed number of atoms.

4. Describe the formation of covalent bonds in molecules including CH₃OH, C₂H₄, O₂, CO₂ and N₂. Use dot-and-cross diagrams...

Covalent bonds can be single, double, or triple:

  • Single Bond: One shared pair (e.g., H-H, C-H, H-Cl).
  • Double Bond: Two shared pairs (e.g., O=O in O₂, C=O in CO₂, C=C in C₂H₄).
  • Triple Bond: Three shared pairs (e.g., N≡N in N₂).

3. Describe in terms of structure and bonding the properties of simple molecular compounds: (a) low melting points and boiling points (b) poor electrical conductivity
  • (a) Low Melting/Boiling Points: Simple molecular substances (e.g., H₂O, CO₂, CH₄) have low MP/BPs because only weak forces between the molecules (intermolecular forces) need to be overcome, not the strong covalent bonds within the molecules.
  • (b) Poor Electrical Conductivity: They do not conduct electricity in any state because they consist of neutral molecules and lack mobile ions or delocalised electrons to carry charge.
5. Explain in terms of structure and bonding the properties of simple molecular compounds: (a) low melting points and boiling points in terms of weak intermolecular forces (specific types of intermolecular forces are not required) (b) poor electrical conductivity
  • (a) Low MP/BP Explained: The forces of attraction *between* simple molecules (intermolecular forces) are much weaker than the strong covalent bonds *within* the molecules. Melting and boiling involve overcoming these weak intermolecular forces, which requires relatively little energy.
  • (b) Poor Conductivity Explained: Simple molecular compounds consist of discrete, neutral molecules. There are no free-moving ions or delocalised electrons available to move through the substance and carry an electrical current.

2.6 Giant Covalent Structures

Giant covalent structures (also called macromolecules or giant molecular structures): Substances where a very large number of atoms are joined together by a network of strong covalent bonds, forming a crystal lattice.

1. Describe the giant covalent structures of graphite and diamond
  • Diamond: Each carbon atom is covalently bonded to four other carbon atoms in a tetrahedral arrangement. This forms a rigid, strong, three-dimensional network.
  • Graphite: Each carbon atom is covalently bonded to three other carbon atoms, forming interconnected hexagonal rings in layers. Within each layer, there are strong covalent bonds. One electron per carbon atom is delocalisedFree to move throughout the layer.. There are only weak forces (intermolecular forces) between the layers.
2. Relate the structures and bonding of graphite and diamond to their uses, limited to: (a) graphite as a lubricant and as an electrode (b) diamond in cutting tools

Diamond

Strong 3D covalent network.

  • Property: Very hard, high MP/BP, non-conductor.
  • Use: Cutting tools, drill tips (due to hardness).

Graphite

Layered structure. Strong bonds within layers, weak forces between. Delocalised electrons.

  • Property: Soft/slippery, high MP/BP, conducts electricity.
  • Use: Lubricant (layers slide), Electrodes (conducts electricity via delocalised e-).
3. Describe the giant covalent structure of silicon(IV) oxide, SiO₂

Silicon(IV) oxide (also known as silica or quartz, formula SiO₂): Forms a giant covalent structure similar to diamond.

  • Each silicon (Si) atom is covalently bonded to four oxygen (O) atoms in a tetrahedral shape.
  • Each oxygen (O) atom acts as a bridge, bonding to two silicon (Si) atoms.
  • This creates a strong, rigid, repeating tetrahedral 3D network structure (similar arrangement to diamond, but with alternating Si and O atoms).

SiO₂ Structure (Local Unit)

Tetrahedral Si-O network (repeats throughout structure).

4. Describe the similarity in properties between diamond and silicon(IV) oxide, related to their structures

Both Diamond (C) and Silicon(IV) oxide (SiO₂) have giant covalent structures with strong covalent bonds extending throughout a 3D network. This similar structure leads to similar physical properties:

  • Very hard materials (strong bonds difficult to break/distort).
  • Very high melting points and boiling points (a large amount of energy is needed to break the strong covalent bonds throughout the lattice).
  • Do not conduct electricity (all electrons are localised in covalent bonds, no mobile ions or delocalised electrons).
  • Generally insoluble in water and most other solvents.

2.7 Metallic Bonding (Supplement)

1. Describe metallic bonding as the electrostatic attraction between the positive ions in a giant metallic lattice and a ‘sea’ of delocalised electrons

Metallic bonding: The strong electrostatic force of attraction between a regular lattice (grid-like structure) of positive metal ions and the surrounding 'sea' of delocalised electrons. These delocalised electrons originated from the outer shells of the metal atoms and are free to move throughout the entire metallic structure.

Metallic Bonding Model

Positive ions in regular lattice, surrounded by mobile delocalised electrons.

2. Explain in terms of structure and bonding the properties of metals: (a) good electrical conductivity (b) malleability and ductility
  • (a) Good Electrical Conductivity Explained: The delocalised electrons are free to move throughout the metallic lattice. When a voltage is applied, these mobile electrons drift towards the positive terminal, carrying charge and allowing the metal to conduct electricity (and also heat).
  • (b) Malleability and Ductility Explained: Malleable = can be hammered into shape; Ductile = can be drawn into wires. In a metal, the layers of positive ions can slide over one another when a force is applied without breaking the metallic bond, because the delocalised electrons can move to maintain the electrostatic attraction between the layers. This allows the metal to change shape without shattering (unlike ionic or giant covalent structures).

Chapter 2 Summary

Elements, Compounds, Mixtures

  • Element: 1 type atom. Compound: ≥2 elements bonded fixed ratio. Mixture: Not bonded, variable ratio.

Atomic Structure

  • Atom: Nucleus (protons, neutrons) + electrons (shells). p(1,+1), n(1,0), e(~0,-1).
  • Z=num protons. A=num protons+num neutrons. Neutral: num electrons = num protons. Config (Z≤20): 2,8,8... Group = outer electrons, Period = num shells.

Isotopes

  • Same Z, different num neutrons (different A). Notation: \(^{A}_{Z}\text{X}^{\text{charge}}\). Same chemical props (same electron config). \(A_r = \frac{\sum (\text{mass} \times \%)}{100}\).

Ionic Bonding

  • Metal loses electrons → cation(+). Non-metal gains electrons → anion(-). Strong electrostatic attraction. Giant lattice structure. High MP/BP. Conducts electricity when molten/aqueous (mobile ions).

Covalent Bonding

  • Shared electron pair(s). Simple molecules (H₂O, CO₂, CH₄...). Low MP/BP (weak intermolecular forces overcome). Poor conductor (no mobile charges).

Giant Covalent

  • Network of atoms, strong covalent bonds. High MP/BP. Diamond (C, hard, insulator, cutting). Graphite (C, layers, conducts, lubricant, electrodes). SiO₂ (similar to diamond props).

Metallic Bonding (Supp)

  • Lattice positive ions + delocalised electron sea. Good conductor (mobile electrons). Malleable/ductile (layers slide, electrons adapt).
