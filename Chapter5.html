Chapter 5: Chemical Energetics

5.1 Exothermic and Endothermic Reactions

1: State that an exothermic reaction transfers thermal energy to the surroundings leading to an increase in the temperature of the surroundings
Exothermic Reaction: A reaction that releases thermal energy (heat) to the surroundings. This causes the temperature of the surroundings to increase. Think: Exo = ExitHeat energy EXITS the chemical system.

Common Examples of Exothermic Reactions:

  • Combustion: Burning fuels like methane (\(CH_4 + 2O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + 2H_2O\)) or hydrogen (\(2H_2 + O_2 \rightarrow 2H_2O\)). Gives out heat and light.
  • Neutralisation: Reaction between acids and bases (e.g., \(HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) \rightarrow NaCl(aq) + H_2O(l)\)). Feels warm.
  • Respiration: Biological process releasing energy from glucose.
  • Oxidation Reactions: Many oxidation reactions, like the rusting of iron, are exothermic.
2: State that an endothermic reaction takes in thermal energy from the surroundings leading to a decrease in the temperature of the surroundings
Endothermic Reaction: A reaction that absorbs thermal energy (heat) from the surroundings. This causes the temperature of the surroundings to decrease. Think: Endo = EnterHeat energy ENTERS the chemical system.

Common Examples of Endothermic Reactions:

  • Thermal Decomposition: Breaking down compounds using heat (e.g., Calcium carbonate decomposition: \(CaCO_3(s) \xrightarrow{heat} CaO(s) + CO_2(g)\)). Heat must be continuously supplied.
  • Dissolving some salts: Dissolving ammonium nitrate (\(NH_4NO_3\)) or potassium chloride (KCl) in water makes the solution feel cold. Used in instant cold packs.
  • Photosynthesis: Plants absorbing light energy to convert CO₂ and water into glucose. \(6CO_2 + 6H_2O \xrightarrow{light} C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2\).
  • Electrolysis: Requires electrical energy input to decompose compounds.
  • Reaction between citric acid and sodium hydrogen carbonate.
4: State that the transfer of thermal energy during a reaction is called the enthalpy change, ∆H, of the reaction. State that ∆H is negative for exothermic reactions and positive for endothermic reactions
Enthalpy Change (\(\Delta H\)): The amount of heat energy transferred (absorbed or released) during a chemical reaction carried out at constant pressure. Usually measured in kilojoules per mole (kJ/mol).
  • For Exothermic Reactions: Heat is released, the products have lower enthalpy (stored energy) than the reactants. \(\Delta H\) has a negative value (e.g., \(\Delta H = -572\) kJ/mol).
  • For Endothermic Reactions: Heat is absorbed, the products have higher enthalpy than the reactants. \(\Delta H\) has a positive value (e.g., \(\Delta H = +178\) kJ/mol).

5.2 Reaction Pathway Diagrams & Activation Energy

5: Define activation energy, \(E_a\), as the minimum energy that colliding particles must have to react
Activation Energy (\(E_a\)): The minimum amount of energy that colliding reactant particles must possess in order for a reaction to occur (i.e., for bonds to break and new bonds to form). It represents the energy barrier that must be overcome for the reaction to start.
3: Interpret reaction pathway diagrams showing exothermic and endothermic reactions
6: Draw and label reaction pathway diagrams for exothermic and endothermic reactions using information provided, to include: (a) reactants (b) products (c) enthalpy change of the reaction, ∆H (d) activation energy, \(E_a\)

Reaction pathway diagrams (also called energy level diagrams or reaction profiles) show the change in enthalpy during the course of a reaction.

Exothermic Reaction Profile

Energy is released. Products have less energy than reactants. \(\Delta H\) is negative.

  • (a) Reactants: Starting energy level (higher).
  • (b) Products: Ending energy level (lower).
  • (c) Enthalpy Change (\(\Delta H\)): Vertical distance between reactant and product levels (arrow pointing down, negative value).
  • (d) Activation Energy (\(E_a\)): Vertical distance from reactant level to the peak of the curve (the energy barrier).

Endothermic Reaction Profile

Energy is absorbed. Products have more energy than reactants. \(\Delta H\) is positive.

  • (a) Reactants: Starting energy level (lower).
  • (b) Products: Ending energy level (higher).
  • (c) Enthalpy Change (\(\Delta H\)): Vertical distance between reactant and product levels (arrow pointing up, positive value).
  • (d) Activation Energy (\(E_a\)): Vertical distance from reactant level to the peak of the curve.
Role of Catalysts: Catalysts increase reaction rate by providing an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy (\(E_a\)). They do not affect the overall enthalpy change (\(\Delta H\)). On the diagram, a catalyst lowers the height of the peak but the start and end levels remain the same.

5.3 Bond Energies and Enthalpy Change

7: State that bond breaking is an endothermic process and bond making is an exothermic process and explain the enthalpy change of a reaction in terms of bond breaking and bond making

Energy Changes and Chemical Bonds

  • Bond Breaking: Energy must be supplied to break existing chemical bonds in the reactant molecules. This process requires energy input from the surroundings. Bond breaking is always endothermic (energy absorbed, +ve energy change).
  • Bond Making: Energy is released when new chemical bonds are formed in the product molecules. This process releases energy to the surroundings. Bond making is always exothermic (energy released, -ve energy change).
Explanation of Overall Enthalpy Change (\(\Delta H\)):

The overall enthalpy change for a reaction depends on the balance between the energy required to break bonds in the reactants and the energy released when new bonds are formed in the products.

\(\Delta H\) = Energy taken in to BREAK bonds (Reactants) + Energy released when MAKING bonds (Products)
(Remember bond making energy release is negative)
OR (more commonly used in calculation):
\( \Delta H = \Sigma (\text{Bond Energies of Bonds Broken}) - \Sigma (\text{Bond Energies of Bonds Formed}) \)
(Where Bond Energy values are always positive)
  • If more energy is released making bonds than is required to break bonds (\(\Sigma E_{\text{formed}} > \Sigma E_{\text{broken}}\)), the overall reaction is Exothermic (\(\Delta H\) is negative).
  • If more energy is required to break bonds than is released making bonds (\(\Sigma E_{\text{broken}} > \Sigma E_{\text{formed}}\)), the overall reaction is Endothermic (\(\Delta H\) is positive).
8: Calculate the enthalpy change of a reaction using bond energies
Bond Energy (or Bond Enthalpy): The average amount of energy required to break one mole of a specific type of covalent bond in the gaseous state (measured in kJ/mol). Bond energy values are always positive (endothermic process).

Calculation Steps using \( \Delta H = \Sigma E_{\text{broken}} - \Sigma E_{\text{formed}} \):

  1. Draw the displayed formulae of all reactant and product molecules to clearly identify all the bonds present.
  2. List all the bonds broken in the reactant molecules and multiply the number of each type of bond by its average bond energy (from given data). Sum these values to get \(\Sigma E_{\text{broken}}\).
  3. List all the bonds formed in the product molecules and multiply the number of each type of bond by its average bond energy. Sum these values to get \(\Sigma E_{\text{formed}}\).
  4. Calculate the enthalpy change: \(\Delta H = \Sigma E_{\text{broken}} - \Sigma E_{\text{formed}}\).
  5. Determine if the reaction is exothermic (\(\Delta H\) < 0) or endothermic (\(\Delta H\) > 0).

Bond Energy Calculator for \(\Delta H\)

Use the formula \(\Delta H = \Sigma E_{\text{broken}} - \Sigma E_{\text{formed}}\) using total energies.

Example Calculation: Formation of Ammonia \(N_2(g) + 3H_2(g) \rightarrow 2NH_3(g)\)

Given Bond Energies (kJ/mol): N≡N = 945; H-H = 436; N-H = 391

Bonds Broken (Reactants):

  • 1 x (N≡N) = 1 x 945 = 945 kJ/mol
  • 3 x (H-H) = 3 x 436 = 1308 kJ/mol

\(\Sigma E_{\text{broken}} = 945 + 1308 = \mathbf{2253}\) kJ/mol

Bonds Formed (Products):

  • Each \(NH_3\) molecule has 3 (N-H) bonds.
  • There are 2 \(NH_3\) molecules formed.
  • Total (N-H) bonds formed = 2 molecules x 3 bonds/molecule = 6 bonds.
  • 6 x (N-H) = 6 x 391 = 2346 kJ/mol

\(\Sigma E_{\text{formed}} = \mathbf{2346}\) kJ/mol

Enthalpy Change (\(\Delta H\)):

\(\Delta H = \Sigma E_{\text{broken}} - \Sigma E_{\text{formed}} = 2253 - 2346 = \strong style="color: var(--exo-color);">-93 kJ/mol\)

Conclusion: The reaction is exothermic.
